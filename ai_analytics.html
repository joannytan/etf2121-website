Week 12 Capstone — AI Landscape + Responsible AI

This week there is no new model to build. Instead, we map everything you’ve built onto the broader field of artificial intelligence — and ask what it means to use these tools responsibly.

It is FreshMart’s quarterly strategy meeting. The CFO has just returned from a conference where a speaker said that “companies that don’t adopt AI in the next three years will not exist in ten.” The CMO has read three LinkedIn posts about large language models and has a slide with “Generative AI” in a large font. The Head of IT has put together a deck that uses the phrase “AI-powered” on every other page, usually next to a picture of a glowing brain.

You, the junior analyst, have spent the last six weeks actually building models. Linear regression. Logistic regression. Decision trees. KNN. Clustering. You have run cross-validation, profiled customer segments, and written the report that told the CMO which customers to spend the retention budget on.

Here is the quiet truth nobody in that meeting room knows: you have been doing AI all along.

14.1 12.1 The AI Landscape: Where Your Models Fit

“Artificial intelligence” is not one thing. It is an umbrella term covering a wide family of techniques — some simple, some extraordinarily complex — that all share one goal: making computers perform tasks that typically require human judgment. Your models live inside that umbrella.

The AI Family Tree

Artificial Intelligence — Any system that performs tasks typically requiring human intelligence: planning, reasoning, language, pattern recognition.
Machine Learning — AI that learns patterns directly from data, rather than following rules written by hand. Everything in Weeks 7–11 is machine learning.
Deep Learning — ML using many-layered neural networks. Powers image recognition, speech, and translation. Needs vastly more data than FreshMart's 500 customers.
Generative AI — Deep learning trained to generate new content — text, images, code — by predicting what comes next. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. A fundamentally different goal from your prediction models.

Where your models sit:

What you built ML type Branch
Linear Regression (Week 7) Supervised — predicts a number Machine Learning
Logistic Regression (Week 8) Supervised — predicts a category Machine Learning
Decision Trees (Week 9) Supervised — rule-based prediction Machine Learning
K-Nearest Neighbours (Week 10) Supervised — similarity-based Machine Learning
K-Means Clustering (Week 11) Unsupervised — finds structure Machine Learning

None of what you built is “just statistics.” All of it is machine learning. Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence. When someone in your next strategy meeting says “we need AI for this,” you are now entitled to ask: “What kind? Because I’ve built five.”

14.1.1 Supervised vs unsupervised — what the difference looks like

In Weeks 7–10 (supervised learning), every training row had a known label: a real TotalSpend value, a real IsHighValue status. The model learned by comparing its predictions to those known answers and adjusting.

In Week 11 (unsupervised learning), there were no labels. You told the algorithm to find groups, and it found them purely from patterns of similarity in the data — no “right answer” to compare against.

The plot below overlays both branches on the same 500 customers: cluster membership (unsupervised, colour) and IsHighValue status (supervised, shape).

Both analyses used the same 500 FreshMart customers. The clusters were found with no labels — the algorithm had no idea what “High Value” meant. The IsHighValue shapes came from a supervised model trained on labelled data to predict a specific target. Same dataset, two completely different questions, two branches of AI.

14.1.2 How is Generative AI different?

Your models each have a clear target: predict TotalSpend, classify IsHighValue, assign a cluster. Generative AI does something fundamentally different — it learns to predict the next token (word, pixel, code character) from enormous quantities of text or images. There is no “target variable” in the traditional sense.

This makes Generative AI extraordinarily flexible — it can write, draw, and code. It also makes it extraordinarily hard to evaluate: there is no ground truth for “a good sentence,” and the model has no built-in mechanism to distinguish true from false. It produces plausible outputs, not necessarily accurate ones.

Your regression and classification models are precise, auditable, and fast to train. Generative AI is flexible, creative, and expensive to control. They are tools for different jobs — and knowing which job calls for which tool is one of the most valuable things a business analyst can know.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.1

Q1. A colleague says: “We don’t use AI here — we just do logistic regression.” How would you respond?

Q2. Your manager asks whether ChatGPT could replace the customer churn prediction model you built. What would you say?

Q3. Deep learning can achieve higher accuracy than logistic regression on many tasks. Name one reason you might still choose logistic regression.

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Logistic regression is machine learning, and machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence. “AI” is not just ChatGPT and robots — it is a broad family of techniques that learn patterns from data. The colleague is using a narrower definition than what the field actually uses. Logistic regression has been AI since well before the term became fashionable.

A2. ChatGPT is a large language model designed to generate text. It is not designed for structured tabular prediction tasks like “given these customer attributes, predict churn probability from a CSV file.” For that, a logistic regression or decision tree trained on labelled historical data is far more appropriate — more accurate, faster to run, and far easier to audit. ChatGPT could help you explain the model’s results to stakeholders, but it cannot replace the model itself.

A3. Interpretability, auditability, speed, and data requirements. Logistic regression produces coefficients that tell you exactly which variables matter and by how much, and you can trace any single prediction back to those coefficients. A deep learning model is typically a “black box” — you get a prediction but cannot explain it in human terms. In regulated industries (banking, insurance, healthcare), or any context where a decision must be justified to a customer or regulator, that interpretability is often legally required. Deep learning also needs far more training data than FreshMart’s 500 customers to be reliable.

14.2 12.2 AI Tools Business Analysts Actually Use in 2025–2026

Building models in R is one part of AI in business analytics. There is also a broad ecosystem of AI tools that analysts encounter in practice — often before they write a line of tidymodels code. Here is a practical tour of four categories you will see in your first job.

14.2.1 1. AI code assistants

Tools like GitHub Copilot, Claude, and ChatGPT can write R and Python code from a plain-English description. Given a prompt like “fit a logistic regression in tidymodels to predict IsHighValue from TotalTransactions and AvgTransactionValue”, they typically produce working code — and occasionally subtly wrong code.

✅ Genuinely useful for

  • Getting a first draft of code quickly
  • Explaining what an unfamiliar function does
  • Translating code between R and Python
  • Generating boilerplate (loading data, basic plots)

⚠️ Where they fail

  • They don't know your data or domain
  • They hallucinate confident-sounding errors
  • They can't check whether output makes business sense
  • They can't validate a result against reality

The rule: treat AI code assistants like a fast but overconfident colleague. Always check their work.

14.2.2 2. AutoML platforms

Azure AutoML, Google Vertex AI AutoML, and H2O.ai automatically try dozens of algorithms, tune hyperparameters, and return the “best” model. They automate the mechanical parts of modelling that you did by hand in Weeks 7–10.

What they automate: algorithm selection, feature engineering, hyperparameter tuning, model comparison.

What they don’t automate: deciding what question to ask, cleaning and validating data, interpreting results to stakeholders, and understanding whether the “best” model by RMSE is actually best for the business. AutoML is a faster route to a model. It does not replace the analyst.

14.2.3 3. BI platforms with embedded AI

You used Power BI in Weeks 2–4. Power BI Copilot can auto-generate reports, answer questions in plain English, and surface anomalies: “Sales dropped 18% last Tuesday — the main driver appears to be fewer transactions from Queensland.”

These are real and useful features. But they are implementations of methods you already know — regression for trends, anomaly detection for outliers, aggregation for summaries — wrapped in a natural-language interface. Knowing what is under the hood lets you evaluate their output critically rather than accepting it without question.

14.2.4 4. LLMs as a workflow tool — and how to use them well

The most immediately useful AI tool for most business analysts in 2025–2026 is a large language model (LLM) — Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini — used as a thinking and writing partner:

  • Draft the CMO report from your Week 11 clustering output
  • Explain a confusion matrix in plain English for a non-technical audience
  • Write the SQL query for a dataset you’ve already understood
  • Suggest predictor variables for a regression model
  • Debug R code that is producing an unexpected error
  • Summarise a 40-page research report on customer behaviour

The bridge between your problem and a useful response is a skill called prompt engineering. Before we learn how to do it well, it helps to understand what is actually happening inside the model when you send it a message.

14.2.5 How an LLM works — the short version

A large language model is, at its core, a very sophisticated autocomplete.

It was trained on enormous quantities of text — books, websites, academic papers, code, news articles — and learned to do one thing: “given everything that has been said so far, what word should come next?” It does this by finding statistical patterns across billions of examples of human writing.

That is the fundamental mechanism. The model has no memory between conversations, no real-time access to information, and no concept of truth. It produces the most statistically plausible continuation of your text, based on patterns in its training data.

What happens when you send a prompt

  1. Your message is split into tokens — roughly word fragments or punctuation
  2. The model reads all tokens in your message as a single context window
  3. It predicts the most likely next token, based on patterns from training
  4. It appends that token to the context and predicts the next one
  5. This repeats, one token at a time, until the response is complete

The model is not retrieving stored facts or searching a database. It is generating plausible text, token by token, from statistical patterns.

A useful analogy: imagine hiring an analyst who has read every business report, statistics textbook, and data science blog ever written — but has never worked at a real company, never touched actual data, and forgets every conversation the moment it ends. They are extraordinarily well-read. They can write convincingly about anything. They will sometimes confidently write something incorrect, because they are completing a pattern — not checking a fact.

This explains both why LLMs are powerful (pattern-matching across more text than any human could read) and why they fail in predictable ways (they don’t know your specific data, your company, or what actually happened last quarter).

14.2.6 Why prompting skill matters

The same model, given two different prompts, produces outputs of wildly different quality. A vague prompt forces the model to make assumptions about your context, your audience, and your goal — and it will fill those gaps with generic defaults. A specific, well-structured prompt gives the model everything it needs to produce something genuinely useful.

Vague prompt Specific prompt
What you type “explain my model” “I built a logistic regression to predict whether a FreshMart customer is High Value (TotalSpend > $500). Accuracy = 91%, Sensitivity = 78%. Write two paragraphs in plain English for a CMO with no stats background. Focus on business implications, not method.”
What you get A generic explanation of what logistic regression is A draft CMO summary you can edit and send
Time saved None — you still have to rewrite it 20–30 minutes of drafting

The difference is not luck. It is a learnable skill.

14.2.7 The anatomy of a good prompt

A well-structured prompt typically includes five elements. You don’t need all five every time, but the more context you provide, the less the model has to guess — and guessing is where it goes wrong.

1. Context
Who you are and the situation. "I am a junior data analyst at FreshMart, an Australian supermarket with 500 loyalty members."
2. Role
What you want the AI to act as. "Act as an experienced data storyteller."
3. Input
The specific content the AI will work with — paste the actual numbers, code, or model output. Don't describe it; give it directly.
4. Output format
Length, structure, and audience. "Two paragraphs, plain English, no jargon. Suitable for a CMO with no statistics background."
5. Constraints
What to avoid or emphasise. "Do not recommend a specific vendor. Focus on business implications, not technical method."

14.2.8 Six ready-to-use prompt templates for analytics

The following templates are structured using the five-element framework above. Each one is ready to copy, fill in the bracketed sections with your actual data, and use.

Template 1 — Explain model output to a non-technical audience

I am a data analyst at [company name]. I built a logistic regression model to classify customers as “High Value” (spending > $500 lifetime). The model results on the test set are: - Accuracy: [X]% - Sensitivity: [X]% - Specificity: [X]%

Act as a business communication expert. Write two paragraphs explaining these results to our Head of Marketing, who has no statistics background. Emphasise what the numbers mean for the business — not how the model works. Keep it under 150 words.

Template 2 — Interpret a confusion matrix

Here is the confusion matrix from my binary classification model. The positive class is “High Value customer.”

Predicted: Not High Value Predicted: High Value
Actual: Not High Value [TN] [FP]
Actual: High Value [FN] [TP]

Explain in plain English what each of the four numbers means. Then tell me: which type of error is more costly for a loyalty marketing team, and why? Finally, what does this matrix suggest about where the model is weakest?

Template 3 — Debug R code

I am learning R with the tidymodels package. This code produces the following error:

[paste the exact error message here]

Here is my code:

[paste your code here]

Explain what is causing the error in one sentence. Then show me the corrected code. Do not change any part of the code that is already working correctly.

Template 4 — Suggest predictors for a model

I am building a model to predict [target variable] for [describe the business context]. My dataset contains these columns: [list all column names and a one-line description of each].

Which columns would you recommend as predictors? For each one, explain in one sentence why it might be related to [target variable]. Separately, flag any columns that should NOT be used as predictors — for example, because they would cause data leakage — and explain why.

Template 5 — Write a business recommendation from clustering output

I ran K-means clustering (K=[number]) on [number] customers using [list variables]. The clusters have these profiles:

[Paste the profile table here — avg spend, avg transactions, avg tenure, cluster size]

Act as a marketing strategist. Give each cluster a short, memorable business label. Then write one specific, actionable marketing recommendation for each cluster in two sentences. Do not use statistical jargon. Assume the audience is a Head of Marketing.

Template 6 — Write a SQL query from a plain-English description

I have a database table called [table name] with these columns: [list columns and their data types or descriptions].

I want to [describe what you want in plain English — filter conditions, groupings, sorting, etc.].

Write the SQL query using standard SQL syntax. Add a one-line comment above each clause explaining what it does.

NoteA prompt is a draft, not a final answer

Even with a well-structured prompt, always review the output before using it. Check: Does the explanation match the actual numbers? Did the SQL query capture all the conditions I described? Does the business recommendation make sense given what I know about the customer base? LLM output is a fast first draft — the analyst’s judgment is what makes it reliable.

Tip✅ Solution 
library(tidyverse)
library(tidymodels)

freshmart <- read_csv(
  "freshmart_customers.csv",
  show_col_types = FALSE
) |>
  mutate(IsHighValue = factor(IsHighValue, levels = c("False", "True")))

set.seed(42)
split <- initial_split(freshmart, prop = 0.75, strata = IsHighValue)

lr_fit <- workflow() |>
  add_recipe(
    recipe(IsHighValue ~ TotalTransactions + AvgTransactionValue + DaysSinceFirstPurchase,
           data = training(split)) |>
      step_normalize(all_numeric_predictors())
  ) |>
  add_model(logistic_reg() |> set_engine("glm")) |>
  fit(data = training(split))

augment(lr_fit, new_data = testing(split)) |>
  conf_mat(truth = IsHighValue, estimate = .pred_class) |>
  summary()

Accuracy alone is not enough. Check also:

  • Sensitivity (recall): what fraction of genuine High Value customers did the model catch? Low sensitivity means the model is missing people it should be identifying.
  • Specificity: what fraction of Not High Value customers did it correctly leave alone? Low specificity means wasted discount spend.
  • Business cost: is a missed High Value customer (false negative) more costly than a wasted offer on a non-High Value customer (false positive)? That asymmetry should drive your threshold choice — and possibly your choice of model.
Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.2

Q1. An AI code assistant generates a tidymodels workflow that trains a random forest and reports 94% accuracy. What should you do before using it?

Q2. Power BI Copilot reports: “Sales dropped 15% in Brisbane last month.” What questions would you ask before presenting this to the CFO?

Q3. What does AutoML automate compared to what you did in Weeks 7–10? What does it still not do?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Verify the model is actually doing what you think: check that the recipe has no data leakage, that 94% accuracy is not an artefact of class imbalance, that the predictions make sense for individual customers you know (Marcus Lee should be predicted High Value; Nina Patel should not), and that a random forest is the right choice for this problem rather than just the most impressive-sounding option. High accuracy does not guarantee a trustworthy or useful model.

A2. Is 15% unusual for Brisbane in this period — is there a seasonal baseline? What does “sales” mean — revenue or transaction count? Is the month complete or is this a partial-month comparison? Is it one store or all Brisbane stores? How confident is the anomaly detection that this is a real drop rather than noise in a small market?

A3. AutoML automates algorithm selection, hyperparameter tuning, feature engineering, and model comparison. What it still does not do: deciding what question to ask, understanding data quality and its limitations, interpreting results in business terms, making the interpretability vs. accuracy tradeoff, and communicating findings to non-technical stakeholders. You did all of those in Weeks 7–10.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.2 (Prompt Engineering)

Q1. A classmate sends an LLM this prompt: “explain clustering.” They get a generic textbook definition. They try again with: “I ran K-means with K=3 on FreshMart’s 500 loyalty customers. The three clusters have average spend of $4,200, $890, and $120. Write one marketing recommendation for each cluster in two sentences each. No jargon — my audience is the Head of Marketing.” The second response is dramatically more useful. Which of the five prompt elements most explains the improvement?

Q2. An LLM confidently tells you that the R function step_normalize() normalises using the median and IQR. You know from Week 10 that it uses the mean and standard deviation. Why did the model give you the wrong answer, and what does this tell you about when to trust LLM output?

Q3. You ask an LLM to write a SQL query that returns “all FreshMart customers who spent more than $500 and joined before 2022.” It returns a query with a WHERE clause that looks correct. Before using it, what should you do?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. The combination of Input (the actual cluster profile numbers), Output format (one recommendation per cluster, two sentences each), and Constraints (no jargon, audience is Head of Marketing) made the biggest difference. The first prompt gave the model no data to work with and no specification of what “useful” looks like — so it defaulted to a textbook definition. The second prompt gave it the actual content to reason about and clear boundaries for what the output should look like.

A2. The model is not retrieving facts from a database — it is generating statistically plausible text based on patterns in its training data. Text about normalisation in statistics often mentions both z-score normalisation (mean/SD) and robust normalisation (median/IQR), and the model may have completed the pattern incorrectly. This is a hallucination: a confident, fluent, plausible-sounding response that is factually wrong. The lesson: always verify LLM claims about specific functions, packages, or technical details against the actual documentation. Trust the R help file (?step_normalize) over the LLM.

A3. Test it. Run the query on a small known subset of the data and manually check whether the results match what you expect. For example: find a customer you know joined before 2022 and spent more than $500 (Marcus Lee, CustomerID 501, joined 2021) — check that he appears in the results. Find a customer who should NOT appear (Nina Patel, $0 spend) — check she does not. SQL is easy to test; always test before relying on AI-generated queries in production.

14.3 12.3 Responsible AI: Bias, Fairness, and the Analyst’s Duty

Building a model that works is not the same as building a model that is fair, trustworthy, or appropriate to deploy. This section covers three responsible-AI concepts every business analyst will encounter — illustrated throughout with the FreshMart data you’ve worked with all semester.

14.3.1 Sampling bias

A model is only as good as the data it was trained on. If the training data is biased, the model inherits that bias and then applies it systematically to every future prediction.

FreshMart example: Suppose FreshMart collected its customer data entirely during a pre-Christmas promotional period, when customers spent 40% more than usual. A model trained on this data will systematically overestimate spend for customers acquired in January or July — those customers will look “Not High Value” even if they are loyal, moderate-spending regulars. The model is not broken; it learned faithfully from the data. The data was the problem.

The analyst’s responsibility: understand when and how the training data was collected, and whether those conditions still hold for the customers you are predicting on.

14.3.2 Disparate impact

Even when training data is unbiased, a model can produce outcomes that differ systematically across subgroups — by city, age bracket, or membership tier. This is called disparate impact.

It matters because model output is often used to make real decisions: which customers get a discount offer, which ones get a loyalty tier upgrade, which ones get a churn-prevention call. If the model systematically misses genuine High Value customers in one city, those customers are excluded from opportunities they deserve — and the business decision is unfair, even if unintentionally.

14.3.3 Interpretability vs accuracy

Not all models are equally explainable. The models you’ve built sit on a spectrum from fully transparent to opaque.

The Interpretability Spectrum

Linear Regression
Fully interpretable.
Equation explains every prediction.
Logistic Regression
Coefficients readable.
Log-odds requires care.
Decision Tree
Rules are human-readable.
Deep trees get complex.
KNN
Can explain individual predictions.
No global summary.
Deep Learning
Black box. Needs separate explanation tools.
← More interpretable, less accurate on complex tasks More accurate on complex tasks, less interpretable →

In regulated industries — financial services, healthcare, insurance — an algorithm that cannot explain a specific decision may face legal challenges. The EU AI Act (2024) and Australia’s AI Safety Standard proposals both include explainability requirements for high-risk AI applications. A logistic regression that is 3% less accurate than a neural network, but whose every prediction can be traced to a coefficient, is often the better business choice.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.3

Q1. FreshMart wants to use the logistic regression model on customers at a new store in regional Victoria. What responsible-AI concern does this raise?

Q2. The fairness audit shows Brisbane customers have a 15% higher false negative rate than Sydney customers. A manager says: “That’s fine — Brisbane customers are just lower spenders overall.” Is this a satisfying explanation? What would you check?

Q3. A new deep learning model achieves 96% accuracy compared to 91% for logistic regression. Name two questions you would ask before recommending it for production use.

Tip✅ Answers

A1. The model was trained entirely on metropolitan customers from five cities. It has never seen rural buying patterns, different store density, or different product mixes. Applying it to a new context outside the training distribution is a sampling bias problem — the model may be systematically wrong for exactly the customers it is most needed for. You would need to collect data from the new store before trusting the model’s predictions there.

A2. Not satisfying on its own. You need to check: after controlling for actual spend level, are Brisbane customers still more likely to be missed? If the false negative rate remains high even among Brisbane customers who genuinely are High Value (TotalSpend > $500), then the model is penalising Brisbane customers based on some other correlated feature — perhaps city-level transaction patterns in the training data. That would be disparate impact, not a real spending difference, and it would need to be addressed.

A3. (1) Can the model explain individual predictions? If a customer is denied a loyalty tier upgrade, can we tell them and our compliance team why, in terms they can understand? (2) Is the 5% accuracy gain worth the cost — in computation, maintenance, regulatory risk, and the loss of readable coefficients? A small accuracy improvement from an opaque model is often not worth the compliance and trust tradeoff in a customer-facing business context.

14.4 12.4 What AI Cannot (Yet) Do

AI tools are genuinely powerful for the things they are designed for. They are also genuinely limited in five ways that matter enormously in business analytics.

14.4.1 1. Frame the problem

No algorithm decided that FreshMart needed customer segmentation. No model suggested that predicting IsHighValue was the right target. Those were human judgments — based on domain knowledge, business priorities, and conversations with the CMO. The question “what should we measure?” is still entirely a human question.

14.4.2 2. Know when the data is wrong

Nina Patel (CustomerID 503) has $0 spend and 0 transactions. The model correctly classifies her as Not High Value — but it has no idea whether she is a genuine inactive customer, a duplicate account, or a data entry error from someone testing the loyalty system. The model treats every row as valid. The analyst is the one who notices the anomaly and asks whether it should be there.

14.4.3 3. Translate output into action

The K-means result produces: “Cluster_2 has a WSS contribution of 1,412 and a mean TotalSpend of $2,340.” The CMO needs to hear: “This is your Loyal Champions group — 127 customers who drive 43% of your revenue, and you should be protecting them with exclusive rewards.”

That translation is judgment, narrative, and domain knowledge. Algorithms produce numbers; analysts produce meaning.

14.4.4 4. Be accountable

When a model makes a wrong decision — flags a genuine High Value customer as low priority and that customer leaves — the model cannot be held responsible. A human is accountable: the analyst who built it, the manager who deployed it, the business that used it without oversight. AI does not absorb responsibility.

14.4.5 5. Reason about causation

In the EDA chapter (Week 6), you found that LoyaltyPoints and TotalSpend are strongly correlated (r ≈ 0.85). The logistic regression uses LoyaltyPoints as a predictor. But correlation is not causation — giving every FreshMart customer free loyalty points will not cause them to spend more. The causal arrow almost certainly runs the other way: customers who spend more earn more points. AI models learn correlations; deciding which correlations represent causal relationships, and which would support a real intervention, is still a human reasoning task.

The block below demonstrates one specific limitation: the model produces confident predictions even for inputs it has never seen anything like.

The model predicts a confident classification for customers with negative transactions and $50,000 average transaction values — inputs that are physically impossible and completely outside anything it was trained on. This is not a bug; it is how statistical models work. They extrapolate beyond the training data, often without any signal that they are doing so. The analyst’s job is to prevent nonsense inputs from reaching the model in production, and to question predictions on new data that looks very different from training data.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.4

Q1. Gary McSnarl (CustomerID 502) made exactly one purchase for $34.90 and has not returned. The model classifies him as Not High Value. Is the model wrong?

Q2. FreshMart’s Head of Analytics proposes fully automating loyalty tier upgrades — if the model says High Value, the customer is upgraded automatically, with no human review. What is the strongest argument against this approach?

Q3. A data scientist shows you that increasing TotalTransactions is strongly correlated with higher TotalSpend in the training data. She suggests a marketing campaign to give customers more transaction opportunities (e.g., smaller, more frequent offers). What causal reasoning question should you ask first?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Not wrong — Gary really does look like a Not High Value customer based on his behaviour. But the model doesn’t know why Gary has one transaction: it could be that he genuinely disliked the experience (legitimate prediction), or that he bought once at a checkout and never received his loyalty card (data collection issue), or that his purchase was mis-attributed to the wrong account (data quality issue). The model predicts correctly on his data; the analyst still needs to assess whether that data is accurate.

A2. The model makes mistakes — at 91% accuracy, roughly 9% of classifications are wrong. Some of those errors are false negatives: genuine High Value customers classified as not high value, who would be denied an upgrade they deserve. For those customers, there is no human in the loop to catch the error before harm is done. Additionally, customers have a reasonable expectation of being able to query a decision that affects them. An automated system with no human review point is both operationally risky and increasingly difficult to defend legally.

A3. Does higher transaction frequency cause higher spend, or do high-spend customers naturally transact more? If the causal arrow runs from spend to transactions (customers who spend more simply buy more often), then incentivising more transactions — giving smaller, more frequent offers — may not increase total spend at all. You’d be subsidising behaviour that was already happening. To answer the causal question, you would need an experiment: randomly assign some customers to the campaign and observe whether their total spend increases relative to a control group.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 12.5

Q1. A graduate analyst is asked to “build an AI model to improve customer retention.” Before writing a single line of R, what are the first two questions they should ask?

Q2. You have built a logistic regression (91% accurate) and a KNN model (89% accurate) for the same classification problem. The logistic regression is slower to update when new data arrives but produces readable coefficients. Which would you recommend, and why?

NoteExercise 12.7 — Automated Decisions

FreshMart’s marketing team proposes using the logistic regression model to automatically upgrade — or deny upgrades to — customers’ loyalty tiers, with no human review of individual cases. Tier upgrades come with a $50 benefit; the model achieves 91% accuracy.

  1. Identify two specific risks of fully automating this decision.

  2. The model has 91% accuracy overall. Is this sufficient for automated decision-making in this context? What other metrics would change your assessment?

  3. Propose a policy for how the model’s output should be used — what decisions should be automated, and what should require human review?

Tip✅ Solution

(a) (1) False negatives at scale: with 500 customers and 91% accuracy, roughly 45 customers may be wrongly classified. For High Value customers wrongly denied an upgrade, the error has a direct financial and relationship cost — they may churn precisely because they feel undervalued. With no human review, there is no mechanism to catch these cases before harm is done. (2) Regulatory and reputational exposure: if a customer queries why they were denied an upgrade and is told “the algorithm decided,” FreshMart has no human-readable explanation to offer. Depending on the jurisdiction and context, this may be legally problematic and is almost certainly bad for trust.

(b) 91% overall accuracy is insufficient on its own. More relevant metrics: (1) Sensitivity — what fraction of genuine High Value customers receive an upgrade? If sensitivity is low (say, 70%), then 30% of FreshMart’s most valuable customers are being wrongly denied benefits. That is a serious business problem. (2) Cost asymmetry — a false negative (denying a High Value customer) costs more than a false positive (giving a $50 benefit to a borderline customer). The decision threshold should be adjusted to reflect this asymmetry, not left at the default 50%.

(c) A reasonable policy: (1) Automate upgrades for customers with .pred_True > 0.85 (high-confidence High Value) — these are very unlikely to be false positives. (2) Automate rejections only for customers with .pred_False > 0.90 (high-confidence Not High Value). (3) Route the middle band (prediction probability between 0.50 and 0.85) to a human analyst for manual review. This captures the value of automation on the confident cases while protecting against errors in the uncertain zone where mistakes are most likely.
