Clustering

Every model we've built so far had a target variable to predict. This week there is no target. We ask a more open-ended question: "Are there natural groups of customers hiding in this data?" Clustering finds them without any labels — and the answers often surprise even experienced analysts.

Weeks 7–10 are called supervised learning — every observation in the training data has a known label, and we teach the model using those labels. Week 11 is unsupervised learning — there are no labels, and we let the data reveal its own structure.

13.1 11.1 Supervised vs Unsupervised Learning

Supervised learning (Chapters 7–10): We have a labelled dataset where the “right answer” is known for each training observation. The model learns to predict that answer for new observations.

Unsupervised learning (this chapter): There is no right answer. We’re not predicting — we’re discovering. We want to find structure, patterns, or natural groupings in the data that weren’t defined in advance.

FreshMart’s business question today: The CMO calls you into a meeting and says: “I don’t think all our customers are the same. I feel like we have different types of customers — frequent shoppers, occasional big spenders, people who signed up but barely come back. Can you show me the data on this?”

This is a clustering problem. We’re not predicting whether someone is “High Value” — we’re discovering what types of customers actually exist in the data.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 11.1

Q1. In supervised learning, we use a target variable to train the model. What is used to guide clustering instead?

Q2. After running a clustering algorithm and finding 3 groups, a colleague says “these are the only 3 types of FreshMart customers.” Is this a fair statement? Why not?

Q3. Name one real-world business application of clustering other than customer segmentation.

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Clustering is guided purely by similarity (distance) between observations. Points that are close together in the feature space end up in the same cluster. There is no external label or correct answer — only the structure of the data itself.

A2. No — clustering is a simplification. The number of clusters K is chosen by the analyst, and the resulting groups are the algorithm’s best approximation of natural groupings. With K=3 we find 3 groups, with K=5 we’d find 5. The “true” number of customer types is unknowable; clustering gives us a useful approximation.

A3. Examples: grouping news articles by topic (text clustering), grouping geographic areas by demographic similarity (for store location decisions), detecting unusual transactions (anomaly detection), grouping genes by expression pattern in biology.

13.2 11.2 The K-Means Algorithm

K-means is the most widely used clustering algorithm. It works by repeatedly improving a set of K cluster centres (called centroids):

K-Means: Step by Step

  1. Initialise: Randomly place K centroids in the data space
  2. Assign: Each data point is assigned to its nearest centroid
  3. Update: Each centroid moves to the mean of the points assigned to it
  4. Repeat steps 2–3 until cluster assignments stop changing

The algorithm converges when no points change cluster — usually within 10–50 iterations.

13.2.1 K-means measures within-cluster distance

The quality of a clustering solution is measured by Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WSS): the total squared distance of every point from its centroid. A lower WSS means tighter, more compact clusters.

K-means tries to minimise WSS — it’s looking for tight, well-separated groups.

Important: Like KNN, K-means uses distance — so scaling is essential before running it.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 11.2

Q1. K-means requires you to specify K before running. What is a drawback of this requirement?

Q2. K-means uses distance to assign points to clusters. Why must we scale variables before running it?

Q3. K-means is sensitive to the random initial placement of centroids. Two runs of K-means on the same data with different random seeds can produce different results. How does set.seed() help with this?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. We must guess or search for the right number of clusters before we know the answer. In practice we run K-means for several values of K and use metrics (like the elbow method) to choose.

A2. A variable with a large range (e.g., TotalSpend: $0–$12,000) will dominate the distance calculation over a variable with a small range (e.g., TotalTransactions: 0–65). Scaling ensures every variable contributes equally.

A3. set.seed() fixes the random number generator, so the algorithm always starts with the same initial centroids. This makes the results reproducible — you and a colleague running the same code get the same clusters.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 11.3

Q1. The recipe uses ~ TotalSpend + TotalTransactions + ... with no outcome on the left side of ~. Why is there no outcome variable here?

Q2. Two customers are assigned to the same cluster. Does this mean they are identical? What does it mean?

Q3. We chose K=3 for this initial fit. What information would you use to decide whether 3 is the right choice?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Clustering is unsupervised — there is no variable we’re trying to predict. The ~ notation with predictors on the right side tells the recipe which variables to use for measuring distance. Nothing appears on the left because there is no “target.”

A2. Not identical — they are the most similar group the algorithm could construct given K=3. Within a cluster, customers will vary; they’re just more similar to each other than to customers in other clusters. The cluster is a simplification.

A3. The elbow method — plot WSS (within-cluster sum of squares) against K and look for the “elbow” where adding more clusters gives diminishing returns. We cover this in Section 11.4.

13.4 11.4 Choosing K: The Elbow Method

There is no single “correct” K. We use the elbow method to find a K where adding more clusters stops providing much improvement.

WSS (Within-Cluster Sum of Squares) measures how compact the clusters are. As K increases, WSS always decreases — with K equal to the number of data points, every point is its own cluster and WSS = 0. We want the K beyond which WSS barely improves.

Interpreting the plot: Find the “elbow” — the point where the curve bends and the improvement starts to flatten. If the WSS drops sharply from K=1 to K=3 but barely changes from K=3 to K=4, then K=3 is likely a good choice. Beyond the elbow, extra clusters add complexity without much gain in quality.

NoteThere’s no perfect answer

The elbow is often a judgement call, not a precise point. Two analysts looking at the same plot might choose K=3 or K=4. That’s normal — clustering is exploratory, and the “best” solution is the one that tells the most useful business story. Always ask: do these clusters make sense? Can we name them and use them?

Important✏️ Concept Check — 11.4

Q1. WSS always decreases as K increases. Does a smaller WSS always mean a better clustering? Why not?

Q2. The elbow plot shows a sharp drop from K=1 to K=2, a moderate drop to K=3, and tiny drops from K=4 onwards. What K would you choose?

Q3. Two colleagues both look at the same elbow plot — one suggests K=3, the other K=4. How would you decide between them?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. No. With K equal to the number of data points, WSS = 0 — but 500 clusters of 1 customer each is meaningless for business purposes. We want the fewest clusters that still capture meaningful differences.

A2. K=3 — the point of diminishing returns begins at K=4. Adding a 4th cluster only slightly reduces WSS, meaning the data doesn’t naturally support a 4th distinct group.

A3. Fit both K=3 and K=4, profile each cluster’s characteristics, and ask: does the 4th cluster in K=4 tell a meaningfully different business story, or is it just splitting an existing group? If the extra cluster doesn’t have a clear interpretation or actionable implication, K=3 is preferable.

13.5 11.5 Naming the Clusters and Business Recommendations

Numbers are not a story. “Cluster 2” means nothing to a CMO. After profiling, we give each cluster a name that describes its business meaning — and then propose an action.

Based on the profile output, we can name the clusters. A typical K=3 result on FreshMart data produces something like:

🌟 Loyal Champions

High spend, frequent visits, long tenure. FreshMart's most valuable customers.

→ Action: reward & retain

💛 Growing Shoppers

Moderate spend, building visit frequency, mixed tenure.

→ Action: upsell & grow

💤 Dormant / At Risk

Low spend, few visits, some newly joined and some long-inactive.

→ Action: re-engage

Q1. Cluster names like “Loyal Champions” and “Dormant / At Risk” are invented by the analyst — the algorithm only produces numbers. Why is naming the clusters an important step?

Q2. The “Dormant / At Risk” cluster contains both new customers (few transactions, recently joined) and older inactive ones (few transactions, joined years ago). Is this a problem? What does it suggest?

Q3. FreshMart’s CMO asks: “Can we add a fourth segment for high-spending but infrequent customers?” How would you respond?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Names connect the statistical output to business action. “Cluster_2” cannot be acted on; “Growing Shoppers — upsell & grow” can. Naming is also how you communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders and make the analysis actually useful.

A2. It suggests these two groups may actually be distinct in the data — they just got merged because K=3 isn’t granular enough. Running K=4 or K=5 and re-profiling might separate “recently joined, not yet engaged” from “long-term inactive, at risk of churning” — which would call for different marketing responses.

A3. “Yes — that’s a testable idea. If we run K=4 or K=5 and one of the new clusters matches the profile you’re describing (high spend + low transaction count, i.e., high AvgTransactionValue), then it exists in the data. Let’s try it and see.”

Tip✅ Solution 
# Fit K = 4
set.seed(42)
km_fit_4 <- workflow() |>
  add_recipe(km_recipe) |>
  add_model(k_means(num_clusters = 4)) |>
  fit(data = fm_cluster)

fm_cluster |>
  bind_cols(extract_cluster_assignment(km_fit_4)) |>
  group_by(.cluster) |>
  summarise(
    n             = n(),
    avg_spend     = round(mean(TotalSpend), 0),
    avg_txns      = round(mean(TotalTransactions), 1),
    avg_tenure    = round(mean(DaysSinceFirstPurchase), 0)
  ) |>
  arrange(desc(avg_spend))

Compare the K=4 profile to K=3. Does the fourth cluster represent a genuinely new customer type (e.g., separating “high spend, long tenure” from “high spend, short tenure”)? If yes, it adds value. If it just splits an existing cluster roughly in half with similar profiles, K=3 was sufficient.

NoteExercise 11.3 — Connecting to Earlier Chapters

We spent four chapters (7–10) building supervised models that classified customers into IsHighValue = TRUE/FALSE. Now in Chapter 11 we’ve clustered them without using that label.

Answer these two questions:

  1. Does the “Loyal Champions” cluster roughly correspond to the IsHighValue = TRUE group from Chapter 8? Calculate the percentage of each cluster that is High Value.
  2. Is clustering doing the same job as logistic regression? What is the fundamental difference?
Tip✅ Solution 
# Add IsHighValue back to the clustered data
freshmart_clustered |>
  left_join(freshmart |> select(CustomerID, IsHighValue), by = "CustomerID") |>
  group_by(.cluster) |>
  summarise(
    pct_high_value = round(mean(IsHighValue) * 100, 1)
  )

The Loyal Champions cluster should have a very high % of High Value customers. But the clusters are NOT the same as IsHighValue:

  • Clustering finds groups that are internally similar across all four variables simultaneously — spend, transactions, value, and tenure together.
  • Logistic regression only cares about predicting IsHighValue — it optimises for that single boundary.
  • A customer could be in the “Growing Shoppers” cluster despite being technically “High Value” (if their spend just crossed $500 but their visit pattern is still developing).

They answer different questions: logistic regression asks “is this customer high value?”; clustering asks “what type of customer is this?”

NoteExercise 11.4 — The CMO Report

Write a one-page summary (4–5 short paragraphs) that you would present to FreshMart’s CMO. Include:

  1. What question you were trying to answer
  2. What method you used (in plain language — no technical jargon)
  3. What the three customer segments look like
  4. One specific marketing recommendation for each segment

This is the final exercise of the statistical modelling module — it ties together everything from Chapters 6–11.

Tip✅ Sample Answer

Question: We wanted to understand whether FreshMart’s 500 customers naturally group into distinct types — without assuming in advance what those types should be.

Method: We used a technique called K-means clustering, which places each customer into a group based on how similar they are to their groupmates across four measures: total lifetime spend, number of store visits, average spend per visit, and length of time as a customer. We tested several numbers of groups and found that three was the natural sweet spot.

What we found:

  • Loyal Champions (approx. X customers): Long-term, frequent visitors who consistently spend more per trip. Marcus Lee and Bev O’Brien are in this group. They drive a disproportionate share of FreshMart’s revenue.
  • Growing Shoppers (approx. Y customers): Moderately active customers who visit regularly but haven’t yet hit the highest spend tiers. Priya Sharma and Jake Nguyen typify this segment.
  • Dormant / At Risk (approx. Z customers): Customers with very few transactions and low spend — some newly registered and not yet engaged, others inactive for a long time. Gary McSnarl and Nina Patel are here.

Recommendations:

  • Loyal Champions: Protect and reward. Offer exclusive loyalty tier benefits — early access to specials, personalised “thank you” messages. Losing one of these customers costs far more than keeping them.
  • Growing Shoppers: Invest to grow. Targeted offers on slightly higher-value product categories, gentle prompts to reach the next loyalty tier.
  • Dormant / At Risk: Re-engage or accept churn. A simple “We miss you — here’s $5 off” campaign. If unresponsive after two touch points, focus resources elsewhere.

Limitation: The three segments are a simplification of reality — customers exist on a continuous spectrum. Two customers in the same segment may have very different stories (e.g., Nina Patel never bought; Gary McSnarl tried and left). The model is a useful starting point, not a complete picture.
