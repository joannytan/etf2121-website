1  Database Management System

1.1 A day in the life of a data analyst

Imagine it is your first day as a junior data analyst at a coffee chain with 200 stores across Australia. Your manager walks over with a friendly smile and asks:

“Hey, can you tell me how many large oat-milk lattes we sold in Melbourne last month, broken down by store and by day of the week? Oh, and our regional manager needs it before lunch.”

Where do you even begin?

Every store records every transaction. Every transaction has a time, a barista, a customer, a drink, a size, a price, and a payment method. Multiply that by 200 stores, by tens of thousands of customers a day, by 30 days, and you are looking at millions of rows of data.

If somebody hands you a spreadsheet, your laptop will probably freeze before you can open it. And even if it did open, how would you find just the Melbourne stores, just the large oat-milk lattes, just the past 30 days — without scrolling for hours?

This is the moment a data analyst reaches for a database.

1.2 So… what is a database, really?

A database is just an organised collection of data — but designed so that finding, updating, and protecting that data is fast, safe, and reliable, even when there are millions of records and hundreds of people using it at once.

You already use databases every day, even if you do not realise it:

  • When you log into Instagram, a database checks your password and pulls up your feed.
  • When you tap your Myki card, a database deducts the fare and updates your balance.
  • When Spotify recommends a song, it is querying a database of your listening history.
  • When your bank app shows your latest transaction, that record came from a database — within milliseconds.

A database is a quiet engine running in the background of almost every digital service we touch.

1.3 What is a Database Management System (DBMS)?

A database is the data. A Database Management System (DBMS) is the software that lets people and applications interact with that data — to store it, retrieve it, change it, secure it, and back it up.

Think of it like this:

  • The database is the library — rows of shelves full of books.
  • The DBMS is the librarian — the one who knows where every book lives, lends them out, keeps the catalogue tidy, and stops you from walking off with the rare collection.

Without a librarian, even the best library is just a pile of books.

A DBMS quietly handles a lot of jobs that you would otherwise have to do by hand:

  • Storing data efficiently so it does not take up unnecessary space.
  • Finding data quickly when you ask a question, even across millions of rows.
  • Protecting data integrity so that, for example, a bank balance can never be in two contradictory states at once.
  • Controlling who can see and change what — the intern should not be able to delete payroll records.
  • Letting many people work at once without overwriting each other’s changes.
  • Backing up and recovering data if something goes wrong.

1.4 “But can’t I just use Excel?”

Excel is wonderful. It really is. For small datasets, quick calculations, and simple charts, it is hard to beat. Most data analysts open Excel several times a day.

But Excel was never designed to be a database. The cracks start to show the moment you push past a few thousand rows or need more than one person editing at once.

Picture a real-world example. A bank stores its customer records in Excel. What happens when:

  • Two staff members open the file at the same time and both make edits — whose changes are kept?
  • The dataset grows to 50 million rows — Excel itself caps out at around one million.
  • The file gets emailed around — which copy is the real one?
  • A staff member accidentally sorts only one column, leaving every row attached to the wrong customer.
  • A regulator asks: “Who changed this customer’s address last Tuesday at 3:14pm?”

Each of these is a nightmare in Excel. Each is a routine, solved problem in a DBMS.

Here is the key shift in thinking: a spreadsheet is a document; a database is a system. A document lives on someone’s laptop. A system serves many people, protects the data, and can be trusted as the single source of truth.

1.4.1 Spreadsheet vs. database at a glance

Feature Database (DBMS) Spreadsheet (Excel)
Typical size Millions to billions of rows Up to ~1 million rows, slow well before that
Speed of search Fast, even on huge datasets Slows to a crawl as data grows
Data integrity Enforced by rules and constraints Relies on the user being careful
Multi-user access Many users, simultaneously, safely One file, easily overwritten
Relationships across tables Built-in and powerful Limited (VLOOKUP can only do so much)
Security Fine-grained user permissions Basic password protection
Backup & recovery Automated, often continuous Manual — Save As copy_FINAL_v3
Audit trail Tracks who changed what, and when None by default

A good rule of thumb: if more than one person depends on the data, or if the data is too big for your laptop to enjoy, it belongs in a database.

1.5 The most common kind: a Relational Database (RDBMS)

There are several styles of database, but the one a data analyst encounters most often — by a wide margin — is the relational database, managed by a Relational Database Management System (RDBMS).

A relational database organises information into tables — and that one word is the key to everything.

A table is exactly what it sounds like:

  • Each column describes a property (e.g. customer_id, first_name, email).
  • Each row is one record — one customer, one order, one transaction.

Here is a tiny customers table:

customer_id first_name last_name email city
1001 Aisha Khan aisha@example.com Melbourne
1002 Ben Tran ben@example.com Sydney
1003 Chloe Wong chloe@example.com Melbourne

So far this could be a spreadsheet. The magic of a relational database is that tables can be linked to each other.

Imagine our coffee chain has three tables:

  • customers — who the customer is.
  • orders — what they ordered, when, and at which store.
  • stores — where each store is located.

Each table on its own only tells part of the story. But because the orders table contains a customer_id (which links back to customers) and a store_id (which links back to stores), we can answer rich questions like “How many lattes did Melbourne customers buy on Saturdays?” by combining the tables.

The links between tables are made by special columns called keys:

  • A primary key is a column that uniquely identifies each row in a table — like customer_id in customers. No two customers can share one.
  • A foreign key is a column in one table that points to the primary key of another. In orders, customer_id is a foreign key — it tells us which customer placed the order.

This simple idea — unique IDs that connect tables — is the backbone of almost every business system you will ever work with. Once it clicks, a huge amount of the messy real world becomes tidy.

1.6 Where does SQL come into all this?

You now understand the what (a database) and the who (the DBMS). The missing piece is the how: how do you actually ask a database a question?

The answer is SQL — short for Structured Query Language, and usually pronounced “sequel.”

SQL is the language we use to talk to a relational database. You write a sentence in SQL, the DBMS understands what you want, and it returns the answer. For example, to answer your manager’s coffee question, you might write:

SELECT store_name, COUNT(*) AS num_lattes
FROM orders
JOIN stores ON orders.store_id = stores.store_id
WHERE drink = 'Large Oat Latte'
  AND stores.city = 'Melbourne'
  AND order_time >= '2026-03-01'
GROUP BY store_name;

Do not worry if that looks like wizardry right now — by the end of this section, it will read almost like English. The point for now is: that one short query can sift through millions of rows in seconds. Try doing that in Excel.

1.6.1 Why every data analyst learns SQL

If there is one technical skill that appears in nearly every data-analyst job ad on the planet, it is SQL. Here is why it matters so much:

  • It is the universal language of data. Whether the data lives in PostgreSQL, MySQL, SQL Server, Oracle, Snowflake, or BigQuery, the core syntax is almost identical. Learn it once, use it anywhere.
  • It is where the data lives. Most companies store their important data in relational databases. If you cannot query the database, you cannot do the analysis.
  • It is fast. A well-written SQL query can summarise hundreds of millions of rows in seconds — work that would crash any spreadsheet.
  • It is the entry point to everything else. Power BI dashboards, R analyses, Python models — they all begin with pulling data, and pulling data usually means SQL.

The good news? SQL is also one of the easiest programming languages to start with. It is declarative, which means you describe what you want, not how to get it. You say “give me all customers in Melbourne” and the database figures out the most efficient way to find them.

1.7 A first look at SQL commands

SQL has a small vocabulary, but its words fall into three useful families. You do not need to memorise these now — just notice the shape of the language.

Data Definition Language (DDL) — for defining the structure of the database itself.

  • CREATE — make a new table, view, or index.
  • ALTER — change an existing table’s structure.
  • DROP — delete a table or other object permanently.
  • TRUNCATE — empty a table without deleting the table itself.

Data Manipulation Language (DML) — for changing the data inside the tables.

  • INSERT — add new rows.
  • UPDATE — change existing rows.
  • DELETE — remove rows.

Data Query Language (DQL) — for asking questions of the data. As an analyst, this is where you will spend almost all of your time.

  • SELECT — pick the columns you want to see.
  • WHERE — filter to just the rows you care about.
  • ORDER BY — sort the result.
  • GROUP BY and HAVING — summarise the data into groups.
  • JOIN — combine information across multiple tables.
  • DISTINCT — get rid of duplicates.
  • LIMIT — return only the first few rows.

Most data analysts write SELECT queries every single day, and rarely touch CREATE or DROP. Building tables is more often the job of a database administrator or data engineer. Still, it is useful to recognise these commands when you see them — and we will cover the essentials in the next chapter.

1.8 Getting your hands dirty: practising SQL for free

Theory only takes you so far. The fastest way to get comfortable with SQL is to start writing it. Best of all, you do not need to install anything heavy on your laptop.

In this course, we will use FreeSQL — a free, browser-based SQL editor. You sign up once, log in, and can start writing real queries against a real database immediately.

Try it later: open-source alternatives

If you want to keep learning beyond this course, two excellent free, open-source databases to install on your own machine are:

  • SQLite — the lightest, simplest database in the world. A whole database lives in a single file. Perfect for personal projects.
  • PostgreSQL — the same industrial-strength database used by companies like Apple, Instagram, and Reddit. Free forever.

The SQL skills you build here will work, almost unchanged, on either.

1.8.1 Setting up FreeSQL (5 minutes)

  1. Go to freesql.com.
  2. Click Sign Up in the top-right corner.
  3. Enter your name, email, and a password, then click Create Account.
  4. Log in. You will land in an editor with a sample database ready to query.
  5. Type a query, click Run (or press Ctrl + Enter), and watch the results appear.

That is it. You now have a real SQL playground.

In a nutshell

A database is a structured store of information. A DBMS is the software that manages it. The most common kind, the relational database, organises data into tables linked by keys. To talk to it, we use SQL — the language at the heart of nearly every data analyst’s job.
