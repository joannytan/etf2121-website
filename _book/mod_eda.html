Introduction to R & Exploratory Data Analysis

You've just joined FreshMart's analytics team as a junior analyst. The Head of Marketing has a stack of questions about customer behaviour — but before building any model, your job is to understand the data first. This chapter is about that: loading, inspecting, summarising, and visualising the FreshMart customer dataset in R.

📦 tidyverse 📦 GGally 📦 corrr

8.1 6.1 Why R for Business Analytics?

R is a free, open-source programming language designed for statistical computing and data analysis. It is widely used by data analysts, statisticians, and data scientists across industries — from banking and retail to healthcare and government.

You might be wondering: I can already do calculations in Excel — why learn R?

That’s a fair question. Excel is a powerful tool, and you’ll keep using it. But R can do things that are either impossible or very tedious in Excel:

  • Repeatable analysis. An R script is a complete record of every step you took. Run it again tomorrow on fresh data and you get updated results in seconds. No clicking, no re-doing.
  • Handles large datasets easily. FreshMart processes thousands of transactions per day. R handles millions of rows without breaking a sweat.
  • Publication-quality graphics. The ggplot2 package produces graphics that belong in annual reports, not just in a spreadsheet.
  • Statistical modelling. Regression, classification, clustering — all built-in or one package away.

8.1.1 The tidyverse

The tidyverse is a collection of R packages designed to work together, all sharing a common philosophy: data in, insight out. The key packages we’ll use are:

Package What it does
readr Reads CSV and other data files into R
dplyr Filters, groups, summarises, and transforms data
ggplot2 Creates graphics using a “grammar of graphics”
tidyr Reshapes data between wide and long formats

All tidyverse packages are free, maintained by the Posit team (the company behind RStudio), and run in the interactive code blocks on this page via webR — no installation required.

What is webR?

Every code block on this page is live and interactive. Click the ▶ Run button to execute it — R is running directly in your browser. You can edit any code block and re-run it to experiment. Your changes don’t affect anyone else.

8.3 6.3 Descriptive Statistics with dplyr

Before drawing any graphs or fitting any models, we always start with numbers. The dplyr package makes it easy to calculate summary statistics — even broken down by groups.

8.3.1 The pipe operator |>

In R, the pipe (|>) passes the result of one step into the next. Read it as “and then”:

freshmart |>         # start with freshmart, AND THEN
  filter(...) |>    # filter rows, AND THEN
  summarise(...)    # summarise

This style keeps code readable and matches how we think: “take the data, then do this, then do that.”

8.3.2 Summarising the whole dataset

summarise() computes a summary of the entire dataset. mean(), median(), sd(), min(), max(), and n() are the most useful functions inside it.

Notice that the mean ($414) is much higher than the median ($215). This happens when a small number of high-spending customers (like Marcus Lee with $12,300) pull the average upward. The median — the middle value — is not affected by extreme values and often better represents the “typical” customer.

📚 Recall: Mean vs Median

From first-year statistics: the mean is sensitive to outliers; the median is not. When a distribution is right-skewed (a long tail to the right), the mean > median. This is exactly what we see with customer spend data.

8.3.3 Summarising by group

group_by() splits the dataset into groups before summarising. This is one of the most powerful ideas in data analysis.

8.3.4 Filtering rows

filter() keeps only the rows that match a condition.

Recognise Marcus Lee in the top spot? And Bev O’Brien in the top 5?

✏️ Concept Check — Section 6.3

Q1. The mean spend for Sydney is higher than for Melbourne. Does this mean every Sydney customer spends more than every Melbourne customer? Explain.

Q2. Why might the marketing team prefer to look at median spend by city rather than mean spend?

Q3. Write the dplyr code to find the 3 cities with the lowest median spend. (Hint: change arrange(desc(...)) to arrange(...))

✅ Answers

A1. No. The mean is an average across all customers in that city. Individual customers in Melbourne may spend far more than some Sydney customers. The city average doesn’t tell us about any individual.

A2. A single very high-spending customer (like Marcus Lee in Sydney) can inflate the mean for that city, making it look like Sydney customers typically spend more when most of them might not.

A3.

freshmart |>
  group_by(City) |>
  summarise(median_spend = median(TotalSpend)) |>
  arrange(median_spend) |>
  slice_head(n = 3)

8.4 6.4 Visualising Distributions with ggplot2

Numbers tell us the centre and spread, but a graph tells us the shape. The ggplot2 package builds graphics using the grammar of graphics — a consistent layering system.

Every ggplot has three essential parts:

ggplot(data, aes(x = variable)) +   # 1. What data and which variable?
  geom_histogram() +                 # 2. What kind of geometry (shape)?
  labs(title = "My Plot")            # 3. Labels and styling

8.4.1 Histograms — seeing the shape of a distribution

What does this tell us?

The distribution is strongly right-skewed: most customers cluster in the $0–$500 range, but a long tail extends to the right where a small group of high-spending customers live (including Marcus Lee at $12,300). This is typical for retail spending data.

📚 Recall: Distribution Shape
  • Symmetric: mean ≈ median; bell-shaped
  • Right-skewed (positive skew): long tail to the right; mean > median
  • Left-skewed (negative skew): long tail to the left; mean < median

8.4.2 Log scale for skewed data

When data is this skewed, a log scale can make patterns clearer:

On a log scale, the distribution looks much more symmetric — a useful reminder that the underlying purchasing patterns aren’t as extreme as the raw numbers suggest.

8.4.3 Boxplots — comparing groups

A boxplot summarises a distribution in five numbers: minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum. It’s ideal for comparing groups side by side.

Notice how the “High” category has much more spread (tall box, long whiskers) than “Low” — this tells us that high-value customers vary a lot in how much they spend, while low-value customers cluster tightly in a small range.

✏️ Concept Check — Section 6.4

Q1. The box in a boxplot spans from Q1 to Q3. What is this range called, and what fraction of the data does it contain?

Q2. In ggplot2, what does aes() stand for, and what kind of information goes inside it?

Q3. A histogram for customer ages looks like a bell curve (symmetric). Would the mean or median be a better description of the “typical” customer age? Why?

✅ Answers

A1. The interquartile range (IQR). It contains the middle 50% of the data.

A2. aes() stands for aesthetics — the visual properties mapped to variables in the data: which variable goes on the x-axis, which on the y-axis, which controls colour, fill, size, etc.

A3. For a symmetric distribution, mean and median are approximately equal, so either works. In practice, analysts often report the mean for symmetric data and the median for skewed data.

8.5 6.5 Relationships Between Variables

So far we’ve looked at one variable at a time. But the interesting questions are usually about relationships: does spending more money earn more loyalty points? Do customers who visit more often also spend more per visit?

8.5.1 Scatter plots

A scatter plot shows the relationship between two numeric variables. Each dot is one customer.

There’s a clear positive relationship: customers who visit more often tend to spend more overall. The trend isn’t perfect (there’s scatter around the line), but the direction is unmistakable. This is exactly the kind of relationship a regression model can quantify — something we’ll do in Chapter 7.

📚 Recall: Correlation

From first-year statistics: the correlation coefficient (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.

  • r ranges from −1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive)
  • r = 0 means no linear relationship
  • r = 0.7 is a strong positive relationship
  • r = −0.3 is a weak negative relationship

Correlation measures linear relationships only. A curved relationship may have r ≈ 0 even if the relationship is strong.

8.5.2 Correlation matrix with corrr

The corrr package (part of the tidymodels ecosystem) makes tidy correlation tables easy to read and visualise.

What do we see?

  • TotalSpend and LoyaltyPoints have near-perfect correlation — they were both earned from the same purchasing behaviour.
  • TotalSpend and TotalTransactions are strongly correlated (r ≈ 0.7) — customers who visit more spend more.
  • DaysSinceFirstPurchase has a moderate positive correlation with spend — longer-tenured customers have had more time to accumulate spend.
  • AvgTransactionValue is less strongly related — some customers make fewer but bigger purchases.

8.5.3 Pairwise scatter plots with GGally

For a quick visual overview of all pairwise relationships at once, GGally::ggpairs() is invaluable:

The diagonal shows each variable’s distribution; the lower panels show scatter plots; the upper panels show correlation coefficients. This is the analyst’s “first look” chart — run it before every modelling project.

✏️ Concept Check — Section 6.5

Q1. The correlation between TotalSpend and LoyaltyPoints is very close to 1.0. Does this mean LoyaltyPoints caused the high spend? Explain.

Q2. A scatter plot shows that customers with higher DaysSinceFirstPurchase tend to have higher TotalSpend, but there’s a lot of scatter. What does “a lot of scatter” tell you about the strength of the relationship?

Q3. FreshMart’s CEO says: “Customers who have more loyalty points spend more, so if we give everyone 1000 free points, they’ll spend more.” What’s the flaw in this reasoning?

✅ Answers

A1. No. Correlation tells us two variables move together — but it says nothing about cause and effect. In this case, both variables are driven by the same underlying behaviour (purchasing). Loyalty points are earned by spending, so they naturally correlate — but points didn’t cause the spending.

A2. A lot of scatter means the relationship is not very tight — knowing someone’s tenure doesn’t precisely predict their spend. There’s a general tendency, but many individual exceptions exist.

A3. This is a classic correlation ≠ causation error. High points and high spend are correlated because they both come from the same purchasing behaviour. Giving away free points doesn’t change underlying purchasing habits.

Exercise 6.1 — Exploring a Different Variable

Using summarise() and group_by(), calculate the mean, median, and standard deviation of TotalTransactions broken down by City. Which city has the most variable number of transactions per customer?

✅ Solution 
freshmart |>
  group_by(City) |>
  summarise(
    mean_txns   = round(mean(TotalTransactions), 1),
    median_txns = median(TotalTransactions),
    sd_txns     = round(sd(TotalTransactions), 1)
  ) |>
  arrange(desc(sd_txns))

The city with the highest sd_txns has the most variable number of transactions — a large standard deviation means customers there range widely from very infrequent to very frequent shoppers.

Exercise 6.2 — Finding Our Characters

Use filter() to find the rows for Marcus Lee, Jake Nguyen, and Nina Patel. Display their FirstName, City, TotalSpend, TotalTransactions, and IsHighValue.

✅ Solution 
freshmart |>
  filter(FirstName %in% c("Marcus", "Jake", "Nina")) |>
  select(FirstName, LastName, City, TotalSpend, TotalTransactions, IsHighValue)

You should see Marcus Lee at $12,300 (high value), Jake Nguyen at $340 (not high value), and Nina Patel at $0 (never purchased).

Exercise 6.5 — Outlier Identification

Use filter() to find all customers with TotalSpend greater than $3,000. How many are there? List their names and cities.

Exercise 6.6 — A Better Scatter Plot

Recreate the TotalTransactions vs TotalSpend scatter plot, but this time exclude Nina Patel (who has 0 transactions and 0 spend) and add axis labels that include dollar signs on the y-axis. Does removing Nina change the trend line noticeably?

✅ Solution 
freshmart |>
  filter(TotalSpend > 0) |>
  ggplot(aes(x = TotalTransactions, y = TotalSpend)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.4, colour = "#8FBFCA", size = 1.8) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", colour = "#9B8EC7") +

  labs(
    title = "Transactions vs Spend (Nina Patel excluded)",
    x     = "Total Transactions",
    y     = "Total Spend ($AUD)"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

The trend line should be very similar — one data point at (0, 0) has minimal effect on a 500-row dataset.

Exercise 6.7 — Correlation Interpretation

The correlation matrix shows r = 0.705 between TotalSpend and TotalTransactions. Write 2–3 sentences interpreting this value for a non-technical FreshMart manager. Avoid the word “correlation” — explain it in plain business language.

✅ Solution

Sample answer: “Customers who visit FreshMart more often tend to spend more money overall — but this relationship isn’t perfectly predictable. A customer who visits twice as often doesn’t necessarily spend exactly twice as much; some infrequent visitors spend a lot per trip, while some frequent visitors only pop in for small purchases. Overall though, visit frequency is a reliable indicator of total customer value.”

Exercise 6.8 — Putting It Together

Write a short paragraph (4–6 sentences) summarising the key findings from your exploratory analysis of the FreshMart customer dataset. Imagine you are presenting to the Head of Marketing before the modelling team begins building predictive models. What do they need to know about the data?

✅ Sample Answer

“The FreshMart customer dataset contains 500 customers from seven Australian cities, with Sydney and Melbourne making up the majority. Customer spending is highly right-skewed: most customers spend less than $500 lifetime, but a small group of high-value customers (about 25% of the base) drive a disproportionate share of revenue — Marcus Lee alone accounts for over $12,000 in lifetime spend. The number of transactions a customer makes is the strongest predictor of their total spend (r ≈ 0.70), suggesting that visit frequency is a key driver of customer value. Loyalty points are almost perfectly correlated with total spend, meaning they contain very similar information. One customer (Nina Patel) has registered but never purchased — she may represent a segment worth targeting with a first-purchase incentive.”
