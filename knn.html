K-Nearest Neighbours

Logistic regression (Week 8) found a mathematical boundary to separate high-value from regular customers. KNN takes a completely different approach: no equations, no boundaries — just "find the most similar customers and vote." Simple idea, surprisingly powerful results.

12.1 10.1 The Nearest Neighbours Idea

KNN is one of the most intuitive algorithms in machine learning. When asked to classify a new customer, it does this:

  1. Measure the distance from the new customer to every customer in the training data
  2. Find the K nearest customers (the most similar ones)
  3. Take a majority vote — if most of those K neighbours are High Value, predict High Value; otherwise predict Not High Value

That’s it. No equations to fit, no parameters to estimate. KNN doesn’t learn a model from training data in the traditional sense — it memorises the training data and uses it directly at prediction time.

A concrete example:

Imagine plotting every customer on a chart with TotalTransactions on the x-axis and AvgTransactionValue on the y-axis. A new customer (Chloe) appears at the point (10, $65). We look at her 5 nearest neighbours (K=5):

  • 3 of them are High Value (TRUE)
  • 2 of them are Not High Value (FALSE)

KNN with K=5 predicts: TRUE (High Value) — majority vote.

12.1.1 Choosing K

The number of neighbours K is a hyperparameter — a setting we choose before training.

  • K = 1: predict based on just the single nearest neighbour. Very sensitive to noise — if that one neighbour is an outlier, the prediction will be wrong.
  • Large K: predict based on many neighbours — smoother, less sensitive to outliers, but may blur real boundaries.
  • Best K: found through cross-validation (covered in Section 10.3).
Important✏️ Concept Check — 10.1

Q1. A new customer has 5 nearest neighbours: TRUE, FALSE, TRUE, TRUE, FALSE. With K=5, what does KNN predict?

Q2. With K=1, training accuracy is always 100%. Why is this not a useful result?

Q3. KNN is sometimes called a “lazy learner.” What do you think this means?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. TRUE — 3 out of 5 neighbours are TRUE, so the majority vote predicts High Value.

A2. With K=1, every training point’s nearest neighbour is itself — so it always predicts correctly on training data. But this tells us nothing about how it will perform on new, unseen customers. K=1 massively overfits.

A3. KNN doesn’t build a model during training — it just stores the data. All the “work” happens at prediction time (finding neighbours). Compare this to linear regression which does all its work up front (fitting coefficients) and then predicts instantly. KNN is “lazy” because it defers the computation until you actually need a prediction.

12.2 10.2 Distance and the Critical Role of Scaling

KNN uses distance to measure similarity. The most common choice is Euclidean distance — the straight-line distance between two points in multi-dimensional space.

For two customers with predictors \((x_1, x_2)\) and \((y_1, y_2)\):

\[d = \sqrt{(x_1 - y_1)^2 + (x_2 - y_2)^2}\]

The scaling problem: Imagine we use TotalTransactions (range: 1–65) and DaysSinceFirstPurchase (range: 14–1,460). Without scaling, a difference of 1 transaction contributes the same as a difference of 1 day — but \(1,460 days\) worth of range dwarfs \(64 transactions\) worth, dominating the distance calculation entirely. A predictor with a large range will overwhelm one with a small range.

The fix: step_normalize() rescales every predictor to have mean = 0 and SD = 1 before computing distances. After normalisation, a 1-unit difference is always 1 standard deviation — every variable is on the same footing.

After normalisation: every variable has mean ≈ 0 and SD ≈ 1. Now a 1-unit change in each variable represents the same relative shift.

Important✏️ Concept Check — 10.2

Q1. Without scaling, DaysSinceFirstPurchase has a much larger range than TotalTransactions. Which variable will dominate the distance calculation — and is this desirable?

Q2. After step_normalize(), what are the mean and standard deviation of each numeric predictor?

Q3. Does step_normalize() change the shape of each variable’s distribution? Explain.

Tip✅ Answers

A1. DaysSinceFirstPurchase will dominate — a 100-day difference will produce a large distance even if the customers have identical transaction behaviour. This is not desirable if we think transactions and days should have roughly equal importance.

A2. Mean ≈ 0 and SD ≈ 1 for every numeric predictor.

A3. No — normalisation shifts and stretches each variable, but the shape of the distribution (skewness, modality) remains exactly the same. It only changes the scale, not the distribution’s form.

12.3 10.3 Fitting KNN and Choosing K with Cross-Validation

We can’t use the test set to choose K — that would be “peeking” at the test data, making our final evaluation dishonest. Instead we use k-fold cross-validation on the training data.

12.3.1 What is cross-validation?

The training data is split into V equal-sized “folds.” The model is trained V times — each time using V−1 folds for training and 1 fold for validation. The final metric is the average across all V folds.

5-Fold Cross-Validation (V = 5)

Round 1: [VAL] [train] [train] [train] [train] → accuracy₁
Round 2: [train] [VAL] [train] [train] [train] → accuracy₂
Round 3: [train] [train] [VAL] [train] [train] → accuracy₃
Round 4: [train] [train] [train] [VAL] [train] → accuracy₄
Round 5: [train] [train] [train] [train] [VAL] → accuracy₅
CV accuracy = mean(accuracy₁ … accuracy₅)

Important✏️ Concept Check — 10.3

Q1. Why do we use cross-validation to choose K rather than using the test set?

Q2. In 5-fold CV, each data point appears in the validation set exactly once. How many times does each point appear in a training set?

Q3. The CV accuracy plot peaks at K=7, then slowly decreases. What does this tell us about K=7 vs K=15?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Using the test set to choose K is a form of “data leakage” — we’d be optimising based on the test data, making our final test-set evaluation dishonest. Cross-validation uses only the training data, keeping the test set truly unseen.

A2. Each data point appears in a training set 4 times (in V-1 = 4 of the 5 rounds).

A3. K=7 achieves the best balance between bias and variance on the training folds. K=15 averages over too many neighbours, blurring the decision boundary and reducing accuracy. K=7 is the “sweet spot” for this dataset.

12.4 10.4 KNN vs Logistic Regression — A Direct Comparison

We’ve now solved the same problem (classify IsHighValue) with two different methods. Let’s compare them directly on the test set.

Discussion: The two models may perform similarly — this is common with well-tuned KNN vs logistic regression on clean data. The key question is not just performance, but also:

Logistic Regression KNN
Interpretability High — coefficients explain the model Low — no equation, just “look at neighbours”
Prediction speed Fast — just apply the equation Slower — scan all training data
Handles non-linearity Poor (assumes linear boundary) Well (flexible boundary)
Needs scaling Helpful but not critical Essential
Important✏️ Concept Check — 10.4

Q1. KNN achieves higher accuracy than logistic regression on this dataset. A colleague says “then we should always use KNN.” What’s wrong with this reasoning?

Q2. A FreshMart manager asks: “Why did the model predict that this customer would be high-value?” How would you answer this question for KNN vs logistic regression?

Q3. We didn’t include City as a predictor in the KNN recipe. Why might including categorical variables be more complicated for KNN?

Tip✅ Answers

A1. Accuracy on one dataset doesn’t mean “always best.” KNN may be slower on large datasets, harder to explain to stakeholders, and may not generalise as well if the data changes. Model choice depends on accuracy, speed, interpretability, and business context.

A2. For logistic regression: “The model assigned higher probability because the customer has made 18 transactions and their coefficient for TotalTransactions is positive — frequent visitors are more likely to be high-value.” For KNN: “The 7 most similar customers in our training data were mostly high-value. Similarity is measured by visit frequency and average spend per trip.” The logistic regression answer is more satisfying for audit purposes.

A3. KNN relies on distance — and distance isn’t naturally defined for categorical variables (e.g., what is the “distance” between Sydney and Melbourne?). We need to either encode categories as dummy variables and be careful about their scale, or use a different distance metric. For this reason, simpler KNN implementations often work best with numeric predictors only.

NoteExercise 10.1 — Manual KNN

The table below shows five training customers. Using only TotalTransactions and AvgTransactionValue (already normalised), calculate the Euclidean distance from a new customer with TotalTransactions = 0.5 and AvgTransactionValue = 0.8 to each training customer. Predict the class using K=3.

Customer TotalTransactions AvgTxnValue IsHighValue
A −1.2 −0.9 FALSE
B 0.4 0.7 TRUE
C 1.8 1.5 TRUE
D −0.5 0.2 FALSE
E 0.6 0.9 TRUE
Tip✅ Solution

Euclidean distance from new customer (0.5, 0.8):

  • To A: √((0.5−(−1.2))² + (0.8−(−0.9))²) = √(1.7² + 1.7²) = √(2.89 + 2.89) = √5.78 ≈ 2.40
  • To B: √((0.5−0.4)² + (0.8−0.7)²) = √(0.01 + 0.01) = √0.02 ≈ 0.14
  • To C: √((0.5−1.8)² + (0.8−1.5)²) = √(1.69 + 0.49) = √2.18 ≈ 1.48
  • To D: √((0.5−(−0.5))² + (0.8−0.2)²) = √(1.00 + 0.36) = √1.36 ≈ 1.17
  • To E: √((0.5−0.6)² + (0.8−0.9)²) = √(0.01 + 0.01) = √0.02 ≈ 0.14

3 nearest: B (0.14), E (0.14), D (1.17) → Labels: TRUE, TRUE, FALSE → Predict: TRUE

NoteExercise 10.2 — The Effect of K

Using the knn_tune results object from Section 10.3, extract and display the cross-validated sensitivity (not just accuracy) for K = 1, 5, 10, 15, and 20. Which K maximises sensitivity? Is it the same K that maximises accuracy?

Tip✅ Solution 
collect_metrics(knn_tune) |>
  filter(.metric == "sensitivity", neighbors %in% c(1, 5, 10, 15, 20)) |>
  select(neighbors, mean, std_err) |>
  arrange(neighbors)

The K that maximises accuracy and the K that maximises sensitivity may differ. For FreshMart’s loyalty campaign, sensitivity matters most — flag as many genuine high-value customers as possible, even at the cost of some false positives.

NoteExercise 10.4 — Model Selection Memo

You are presenting to FreshMart’s Head of Analytics. Write a short recommendation (3–4 sentences) on whether to deploy logistic regression or KNN for the high-value customer identification task. Justify your choice using at least two criteria (e.g., accuracy, interpretability, speed, ease of retraining).

Tip✅ Sample Answer

“Both models achieve similar accuracy on the test set. We recommend logistic regression for production deployment for two reasons: first, when a customer or manager asks why a particular customer was flagged, we can explain it clearly using the coefficients (e.g., high visit frequency is the strongest signal). Second, logistic regression predicts almost instantly because it’s just a formula — KNN requires scanning all training customers at prediction time, which becomes slow as the customer base grows. If interpretability is ever challenged by customers or regulators, logistic regression is easier to defend.”
